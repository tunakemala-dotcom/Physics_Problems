# Task 01 – Coulomb's Law

## Problem Statement

Four point charges of $+1.0 \text{ C}$ each are placed at the corners of a square with sides of $1.0 \text{ m}$. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the electric force on a charge of $-2.0 \text{ C}$ placed at the center of the square.

## Theory

According to Coulomb's Law, the electric force between two point charges is proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The principle of superposition states that the net force on a charge is the vector sum of the individual forces from all other charges.

$$
\vec{F} = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

Let the square lie in the $xy$-plane, centered at the origin $(0,0)$. The four corners are at $(\pm a, \pm a)$, where $2a = 1.0 \text{ m}$. 
The distance from each corner to the center is the same for all four charges.

Let the central charge be $q_0 = -2.0 \text{ C}$ and the corner charges be $q_1 = q_2 = q_3 = q_4 = +1.0 \text{ C}$.

The force exerted by the charge at the top-right corner on the central charge points towards the top-right corner (since opposite charges attract).
The force exerted by the charge at the bottom-left corner on the central charge points towards the bottom-left corner.

Because the corner charges are identical in magnitude and sign, and they are located at equal distances on opposite sides of the central charge, their force vectors are equal in magnitude but exactly opposite in direction.

$$
\vec{F}_{net} = \vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 + \vec{F}_3 + \vec{F}_4
$$

By symmetry, the pairwise opposite forces cancel each other out:

$$
\vec{F}_1 = -\vec{F}_3
$$

$$
\vec{F}_2 = -\vec{F}_4
$$

Therefore:

$$
\vec{F}_{net} = 0
$$

## Final Result

* Magnitude of the electric force: $0 \text{ N}$
* Direction: Undefined (the net force is zero).

## Interpretation

The high degree of symmetry in the charge distribution results in a complete cancellation of the attractive forces pulling the central charge toward the corners. The center of the square is a point of unstable equilibrium for the negative charge.
