# Task 05 – Inelastic Collision

## Problem Statement

A 70 kg runner moving at $3 \text{ m/s}$ jumps onto a 140 kg stationary cart. What is the final speed of the cart with the runner? Is kinetic energy conserved in this collision? Explain.

## Theory

When two objects collide and move together, the collision is perfectly inelastic. 

Total momentum is conserved in all isolated collisions:

$$
\sum \vec{p}_{initial} = \sum \vec{p}_{final}
$$

Kinetic energy is not conserved in inelastic collisions. The loss in kinetic energy is dissipated as heat, sound, or deformation energy.

## Step-by-Step Solution

### Final Speed

Let $m_1 = 70 \text{ kg}$, $v_1 = 3 \text{ m/s}$, $m_2 = 140 \text{ kg}$, and $v_2 = 0 \text{ m/s}$.

By conservation of momentum:

$$
m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v_f
$$

$$
(70)(3) + 0 = (70 + 140) v_f
$$

$$
210 = 210 v_f
$$

$$
v_f = 1 \text{ m/s}
$$

### Checking Kinetic Energy

Calculate initial kinetic energy ($KE_i$):

$$
KE_i = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_1^2 + 0 = \frac{1}{2}(70)(3)^2 = 35(9) = 315 \text{ J}
$$

Calculate final kinetic energy ($KE_f$):

$$
KE_f = \frac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2) v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}(210)(1)^2 = 105 \text{ J}
$$

Calculate the difference:

$$
\Delta KE = KE_f - KE_i = 105 - 315 = -210 \text{ J}
$$

## Final Result

* Final speed: $1 \text{ m/s}$
* Kinetic energy is **not** conserved; exactly $210 \text{ J}$ is lost.

## Interpretation

In a perfectly inelastic collision, momentum is strictly conserved, forcing a massive drop in the system's overall speed. Because kinetic energy depends on the square of velocity, this velocity reduction leads to a disproportionate loss of kinetic energy, which is dissipated (e.g., through the runner's muscles and friction absorbing the impact).
