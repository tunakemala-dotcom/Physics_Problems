# Task 02 – String Harmonics

## Problem Statement

A guitar string is $64 \text{ cm}$ long and has a fundamental frequency (one antinode) of $330 \text{ Hz}$. What is the speed of the wave on this string?

## Theory

For a string fixed at both ends, standing waves are formed. The fundamental frequency (first harmonic) consists of a single antinode between the two nodes at the fixed ends. This corresponds to half a wavelength fitting within the length $L$ of the string:

$$
L = \frac{\lambda}{2}
$$

The wave speed is related to wavelength and frequency by $v = \lambda f$.

## Step-by-Step Solution

Convert the length to standard SI units:

$$
L = 64 \text{ cm} = 0.64 \text{ m}
$$

Calculate the wavelength of the fundamental mode:

$$
\lambda = 2L = 2(0.64) = 1.28 \text{ m}
$$

Calculate the wave speed using the fundamental frequency $f = 330 \text{ Hz}$:

$$
v = \lambda f = (1.28)(330)
$$

$$
v = 422.4 \text{ m/s}
$$

## Final Result

The speed of the wave on the guitar string is $422.4 \text{ m/s}$.

## Interpretation

The wave speed on the string is determined by the string's physical properties (tension and linear mass density). Plucking the string excites this wave speed, establishing a standing wave whose geometry enforces the $330 \text{ Hz}$ tone.
